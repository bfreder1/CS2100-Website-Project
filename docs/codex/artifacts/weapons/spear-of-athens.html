The Spear of Athens

The Spear of Athens is a legendary weapon from early rise of Athens' power. It channels the flames of war with the precision of a master tactician. Historical records indicate Athena herself gifted the spear to her adopted son, Erichthonius, as a show of her favor. As king, Erichthonius built Athens into a powerful city-state, and served as one of many symbols of Athena's protection over the city. Many kings and generals have wielded the Spear of Athens in battle, and its power has been felt across the Grecian Realms. After the collapse of the monarchy and the clergy of Athena, the spear was lost to history. While democracy reigned in Athens, the Aegis became the new great symbol of Athena's protection. The Spear of Athens remains a powerful artifact, and rumors suggest that it has resurfaced in the hands of a new hero.

Historians recently discovered that the spear was forged by Hephaestus himself, then blessed by Athena with the power to channel the flames of war. Athena forced Hephaestus to forge this spear as recompense for his attempted assault on her. This assault resulted in the birth of Erichthonius, whom Athena adopted as her own. Exactly when the spear was forged and when it was gifted to Erichthonius is unknown, but it is believed to have been sometime before Erichthonius' deposition of King Amphictyon. With the spear in hand, Erichthonius both deposed Amphictyon and consolidated power in Athens.

The last known wielder of the Spear of Athens was Pandion II, who ruled Athens before the construction of the Trojan Walls. The spear was lost after he fell into a deep madness and fled the city. Scholars believe that the weakening clergy and ongoing secularization of Athens angered Athena who cursed the sitting king. Pandion's flight from the city caused a schism in the city's leadership during which the spear was lost. The new democracy that emerged from the chaos decided that peace and protection were more valuable tenets than war and conquest. The Aegis then became the new symbol of Athena's favor and little effort was made to recover the Spear of Athens.

First Rediscovery

Scholars documented the first rediscovery of the Spear of Athens in the ruins of an ancient temple to Athena. The spear was found in the hands of a minor statue to Athena, but exact circumstances of its rediscovery are debated. The temple's purpoted location was not known to have been a site of worship for Athena, and the spear was not mentioned in any historical records of the temple. Furthermore, the only primary source documenting the rediscovery was a memoir from a traveling cleric who regularly visited the temple. The cleric's memoir was published posthumously after their disappearance following an attempt to track down the would-be claimant.

The memoir itself features the most detailed depiction of the spear to date, and the magical properties described in the memoir match those from historical records. Skeptical scholars argue that the cleric not taking the spear with them or drawing any attention to the rediscovery is evidence of the memoir's fabrication. An investigation into the temple itself revealed that the spear had indeed been present at the temple for some time, but the exact circumstances of its arrival and departure remain unknown. The spear itself and the cleric were not found during the investigation and further attempts to locate either were deemed too dangerous.


Last Account

The latest account of the Spear of Athens comes from merchants traveling through the city of Phaethon. They reported that the mysterious castle owner, known only as the Alchemist, had come into possession of a powerful spear matching the description of the Spear of Athens. Little is known about the Alchemist, but the merchants reported that on their visit to the castle, the Alchemist possessed a variety of magical artifacts one of which was the spear in question. The merchants were unable to provide any further details about the spear or the Alchemist, but they did report that the castle was heavily fortified and that the Alchemist was a recluse. Petitioners seeking the rumors of the spear were either turned away or never seen again. Scholars were unable to verify the merchants' account, but a party of adventurers were recently seen leaving the Alchemist's castle with a spear in tow. These adventurers passed through Phaethon and eyewitnesses report that the spear was indeed the Spear of Athens. With Athens having been destroyed by the Lich King, it is unknown what the adventurers plan to do with the spear. Hopeful former citizens of Athens believe that the spear can slay the Lich King and bring peace to the Realms once more.

The Spear of Athens is a red lance with a flame-colored tip. The shaft is made of a silver alloy that is both light and strong. Its lightweight design allows the wielder to use the lance as a traditional spear without sacrificing speed or power. The enchantments on the spear make it both more precise and more lethal than a traditional spear. Historical accounts of the spear in battle describe its wielders conjuring lances of fire to strike down their enemies. These flaming lances pierce through armor and shield alike, channeling Athena's wrath to smite her enemies.
